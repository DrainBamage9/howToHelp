# 15.4 Applications of Double Integrals

Double integrals are often used to compute physical quantities. 

## Mass

Given the density function of a quality. This could be the literal density or something like the charge density. This could also be something like grains of sand per unit area. We can find the total mass(m) or total charge(Q).

$$\rho(x,y)=lim\frac{\Delta M}{\Delta A}$$

$$m=\int\int_R\rho(x,y)\ dA$$

Note, we do not need to multiply by a variable here like we did with polar area integrals. That is a polar thing not an area thing. 

## Center of Mass, Moments

We can also find the center of mass ($\bar{x},\bar{y}$), and the moment about an axis ($M_x,M_y$) and the relation between them.

$$M_x=\int\int_D y\ \rho(x,y)\ dA$$
$$M_y=\int\int_D x\ \rho(x,y)\ dA$$

To convert from moment about an axis to the center of mass, simply divide by the total mass (m) found from the previous equation.

$$\bar{x}=\frac{M_y}{m}=\frac1m \int\int_D x\ \rho(x,y)\ dA$$
$$\bar{y}=\frac{M_x}{m}=\frac1m \int\int_D y\ \rho(x,y)\ dA$$

## Moment of Inertia
The moment of inertia about the x-axis is 
$$I_x = \int\int_Dy^2\rho(x,y)\ dA$$
The moment of inertia about the y-axis is 
$$I_y = \int\int_Dx^2\rho(x,y)\ dA$$
We can also find the moment of inertia round the origin, referred to as the polar moment of inertia:
$$I_0=\int\int_D(x^2+y^2)\rho(x,y)\ dA$$
It is also worth noting that $I_0=I_x+I_y$

### Radius of Gyration

This is kinda goofy but is mentioned. The radius of gyration is defined as the distance you could put a point mass to produce the same moment of inertia as the disk. Defined as:
$$mR^2=I$$
Where R is the radius, its easier to define as:
$$\bar{\bar y}^2=\frac{I_x}{m}\quad\quad\bar{\bar x}^2=\frac{I_y}{m}$$

## Probability

This is hard to explain without an example. A primary concept is that the sum of a probability is always equal to one.
$$\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(x,y)\ dx\ dy = 1$$

We also have a special kind of probability function we can find, but there are conditions. If we have a random variable X with a probability function $f_1(x)$ and Y with the probability function $f_2(y)$. Then X and Y are called independent random variables if their join function is a product of the indiviual functions.
$$f(x,y)=f_1(x)f_2(y)$$
We can then model a _wait time_ by using exponential decay. A wait time, $\mu$, is defined as an amount of time it takes for an event to happen (It takes an average of 5 minutes for an event to happen, etc)

$$f(x)=\left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
    0\quad\quad\quad\ \ \  if\ x<0&\\
    \mu^{-1}e^{-x/\mu}\quad if\ x\ge0
    \end{array}
    \right.
$$

We can also find the expected value of a probabilty function using the equation.
$$\mu=\int\int_{R^2}x\ f(x,y)\ dA$$

### Example
The average wait time to buy a ticket is 10 minutes, the average wait time to buy a snack is 5 minutes. If these wait times are independant, what is the probability you wait less than 20 minutes.

$$f_1(x)=\left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
    0\quad\quad\quad\ \ \ \  if\ x<0&\\
    \frac1{10}e^{-x/10}\quad if\ x\ge0
    \end{array}
    \right.
$$

$$f_2(y)=\left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
    0\quad\quad\quad\ if\ y<0&\\
    \frac15e^{-y/5}\quad if\ y\ge0
    \end{array}
    \right.
$$
Since theyre indepenant you can combine:
$$f(x,y) = f_1(x)f_2(y)$$
$$f(x,y) = \left\{
    \begin{array}{l}
    \frac1{50}e^{-x/10}e^{-y/5}\quad if\ x\ge0,y\ge0 \\
    0\quad otherwise
    \end{array}
    \right.
$$
Now find the prob that we wait less than 20 min
$$P(X+Y<20)=\int^{20}_0\int^{20-x}_0\frac1{50}e^{-x/10}e^{-y/5}\ dy\ dx$$
$$\approx.7476$$
