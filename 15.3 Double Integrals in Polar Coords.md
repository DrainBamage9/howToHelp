#15.3 Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

Polar coordinates are useful when the region is circular.
$$x=r\ cos\ \theta$$
$$y=r\ sin\ \theta$$
Also:
$$x^2+y^2=r^2$$
Area Elements become:
$$dA=r\ dr\ d\theta$$
Therefor the integral becomes:
$$\int\int_R f(x,y)dA = \int\int f(r\ cos\theta,\ r\ sin\theta)r\ dr\ d\theta$$
$$\int\int_Rf(r,\theta)\ dA =\int\int_Rf(r,\theta)r\ dr\ d\theta$$
Note the $r$ on the outside of the function, this is important. 

## Exam hack
Use polar when you see:

- $x^2+y^2$
- Circles
- Disks
- Radial

This makes things far easier.

## Example

Evaluate:
$$\int\int_R(x^2+y^2)\ dA$$
Where $R$ is a unit disk.

Step 1. Convert to polar
$$x^2+y^2=r^2$$

Step 2. Find limits.

$R$ is a **unit** disk, meaning the radius is 1. Therefor:

$$0\le r\le 1$$
$$0\le\theta\le2\pi$$

Step 3. Substitute in limits and equation.

$$\int^{2\pi}_0\int^1_0r^2(r)\ dr\ d\theta$$
$$=\int^{2\pi}_0\int^1_0r^3\ dr\ d\theta$$

Step 4. Integrate
Inner:
$$\int^1_0r^3\ dr$$
$$=\frac{r^4}{4}|^1_0$$
$$=\frac14$$
Outer:
$$\int^{2\pi}_0\frac14\ d\theta$$
$$=\frac{2\pi}{4}=\frac\pi2$$
