### Max and Min Values
Find where all partial derivatives are 0:
$$f_x=0,\quad f_y=0$$
These are the crital points. 

Use the second-derivative test to classiy local maxima and minima

Unlike 1-D functions, a function can curve up in one direction and down in another at the same point to form a saddle point. This looks like a pringle chip.

#### Example

$$f(x,y)=x^2+y^2-4x-6y$$
Step 1. Find partial derivatives
$$f_x=2x-4$$
$$f_y=2y-6$$
Step 2. Set equal to zero and solve
$$0=2x-4\rArr x=2$$
$$0=2y-6\rArr y=3$$
This means we have a critical point at (2,3)

Step 3. Second Deriv Test
$$f_{xx}=2,\quad f_{yy}=2,\quad f_{xy}=0$$
Compute Determinant
$$D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-(f_{xy})^2=4$$
Since D>0 and f_{xx} > 0 local minima a (2,3)


### Quick Classification
$$D>0, f_{xx}>0\quad Local \ min$$
$$D>0, f_{xx}<0\quad Local \ max$$
$$D<0\quad Saddle \ point$$
$$D=0, \quad Inconclusive$$
