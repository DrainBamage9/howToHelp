### Directional Derivatives and the Gradiant

#### Directional Derivative

Gives the rate at which $f(x,y)$ changes in any direction, not just along an axis. 

if $\overrightarrow{u}$ is a unit vector then
$$D_uf=\nabla f \cdot \overrightarrow{u}$$
which is equivilent to
$$D_uf=f_x\overrightarrow{u_1}+f_y\overrightarrow{u_2}$$

#### Gradient Vector

The vector of partials:
$$\nabla f = ⟨f_x,f_y⟩ = ⟨\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}⟩$$
point in the direction of the greatest increase of the function.

Also:
- the gradient is perp to the level curves/surfaces.
- its magnitude measures the steepest rate of change.

#### Example
$$f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$$
Find directional derivative at (1,1) towards vector ⟨3,4⟩.

Step 1. Find gradient
$$\nabla f = ⟨2x,2y⟩$$
at (1,1):
$$\nabla f(1,1) = ⟨2,2⟩$$

Step 2. Define unit vector

Find magnitude,
$$|⟨3,4⟩| = 5$$
Normalize into Unit Vector:
$$\overrightarrow{u}=⟨3/5,4/5⟩$$

Step 3. Dot Product
$$D_uf=\nabla f \cdot \overrightarrow{u}$$
$$=⟨2,2⟩\cdot ⟨3/5,4/5⟩$$
$$=6/5 + 8/5 = 14/5$$
Directional Div @(1,1) towards (3,4) = 14/5
