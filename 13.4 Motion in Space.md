### Motion in Space: Pos, Vel, Acc

If a partical's positing defined by:
$$p(t)=⟨x(t),y(t),z(t)⟩$$
then it's velocity is:
$$v(t) = p^′(t)$$
While defines its direction of motion and instantaneous rate of change of its position.

Its speed is equal to the magnitude of the velocity:
$$speed = |v(t)|$$
Which is always a positive scalar.

Its Acceleration is the derivative of the velocity or the second derivative of the position:
$$a(t)=v′(t) = p^{′′}(t)$$
Which defines its change in speed and/or direction.

Acceleration can be broken down into its tangential acceleration (changes speed) and its normal acceleration (changes direction).
$$a_T = \frac{d}{dt}|v|$$
$$a_N=\sqrt{|a^2|-a^2_T}$$

The velocity vector can be broken down into a Unit Tangent vector which defines the direction of motion, not the magnitude.
$$T(t)=\frac{v(t)}{|v(t)|}$$

#### Example
The position of particle is defined by:
$$p(t)=⟨t^2,2t,3⟩$$
Find:

- Velocity
- Speed at t=1
- Acceleration
- Tan Acceleration at t=1

Step 1. Find Vel
$$v(t) = p^′(t)$$
$$v(t) = ⟨2t,2,0⟩$$
Step 2. Find Speed
$$|v(t)| = \sqrt{(2t)^2 + 2^2 +0^2}$$
$$=\sqrt{4t^2+4}$$
Eval at t=1
$$|v(1)|=\sqrt{8}=2\sqrt{2}$$
Step 3. Find Accel
$$a(t)=v^′(t)=⟨2,0,0⟩$$
Constant Acceleration
Step 4. Tan Acceleration
$$a_T=\frac{d}{dt}|v(t)|$$
Therefor
$$a_T(t)=\frac{1}{2}(4t^2+4)^{-1/2}\cdot8t$$
$$=\frac{4t}{\sqrt{4t^2+4}}$$
At t=1:
$$a_T(1)=\frac{4}{\sqrt8}=\sqrt2$$

### Trajectory Equations

A particle's motions is given in parametric form. If gravity(acceleration) is assumed to be constant they can be written in form:
$$x(t)=v_0cos(\theta) t$$
$$y(t)=v_0sin(\theta)t-\frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Where $v_0$ is the initial velocity of the particle at t=0, and $\theta$ is the initial launch angle of the particle.

Something to keep in mind when working in a constant gravity environment while ignoring air resistance. Since $v_0$ is constant the cos and sin break it into its x-y components, and since gravity only effects they y position, is not incorporated into its x position. 

While looking at the y(t) equations, its path from a constant value, integrated up to position should be obvious given the $\frac12t^2 g$

This is also the 2D form of the equation. It can often be simplified into a y(x) equation asssuming the launch point is at (0,0).

#### Example

Find:

- The Position Vector
- Trajectory Equation (singular)
- Time of flight
- Max Height
- Range

Assuming:

- $v_0=50ft/s$$
- $\theta = 30\degree$
- $g=32ft/s^2$

Step 1. Break initial velocity into x-y components.
$$v_{0x}=v_0cos\theta = 50cos30\degree=25\sqrt3$$
$$v_{0y}=v_0sin\theta = 50sin30\degree=25$$
Step 2. Write Position Function

$\bold a=⟨0,−32⟩$

Position Equations:
$$x(t)=v_{0x}t$$
$$y(t)=v_{0y}t+\frac12gt^2$$
Therefor:
$$x(t)=25\sqrt3t$$
$$y(t)=25t-16t^2$$
Positon Vector:
$$r(t)=⟨25\sqrt3​t,25t−16t^2⟩​$$
Step 3. Trajectory Equation
Solve for t using x-pos equation:
$$t=\frac{x}{25\sqrt3}$$
x-pos equation is used as its easier to solve for since its linear

Substitute into y(t):
$$y=25(\frac{x}{25\sqrt3})-16(\frac{x}{25\sqrt3})^2$$
$$y(x)=\frac{x}{\sqrt3}-\frac{16x^2}{1875}$$
Which is our answer

Step 4. Find time of flight

We can assume the ground is at y=0 since there is no indication otherwise. So use the y position equation and solve for 0.
$$25t-16t^2=0$$
$$t=\frac{25}{16}\approx1.5625\ seconds$$

Step 5. Max Height

We know then the y-vel = 0 it has reached its maximum point (or minimum but that doesnt make sense for this scenario).

$$y_{vel}(t)=25-32t=0$$
$$t=\frac{25}{32}s$$
Now solve the y-pos equation for this time. (Note: you can assume that the max point is at half the travel time IF the landing altitude is equal to the launch altitude. It is much safer to use the method I am using.)
$$y_{max} = 25(\frac{25}{32})-16(\frac{25}{32})^2$$
$$y_{max}\approx9.77\ ft$$

Step 6. Range

Range is easy, assuming the particle stops on impact with the floor, x(TOF) = Range
$$x(\frac{25}{16})=25\sqrt3(\frac{25}{16})\approx67.7\ ft$$
